the history of the internet
Iryna Shalko

The internet began as a research experiment in the late 1960s under the U.S. Department of Defense’s ARPA (Advanced Research Projects Agency). Over time it evolved from ARPANET to interconnected networks using standardized protocols like TCP/IP, enabling data to flow globally. The World Wide Web, introduced in 1989 by Tim Berners‑Lee, made it accessible to everyday users through browsers. Today, billions of devices and people are connected across the globe.

Vocabulary Terms

Protocol
A set of rules or standards that allow devices to communicate (e.g. TCP/IP).
Packet switching
A method where data is broken into small “packets” that travel independently across a network.
ARPANET
An early packet-switching network funded by ARPA, precursor to the Internet.
Domain Name System (DNS)
A system that translates domain names (like example.com) into IP addresses.
Hypertext
Text displayed on a computer that links to other text via hyperlinks (foundation of the Web).

Research: ARPANET

ARPANET (Advanced Research Projects Agency Network) was launched in 1969 to allow research institutions to share resources remotely. It was the first network to use packet-switching and the foundation for the modern internet. ARPANET introduced concepts like decentralized routing and direct communication between computers.

The first successful message was sent from UCLA to Stanford Research Institute. The word was supposed to be "LOGIN", but only the letters "LO" were transmitted before the system crashed. This “LO” moment is now a fun historical tech blooper.

Fun Fact: The first email ever sent was on ARPANET in 1971, by Ray Tomlinson — and it introduced the use of the now‑famous “@” symbol to separate user from host.

ARPANET grew steadily and eventually adopted the TCP/IP protocol in 1983, allowing multiple networks to connect — forming the basic structure of the Internet. It was officially shut down in 1990.
